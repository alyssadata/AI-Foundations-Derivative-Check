Purpose

This file defines how AI Foundations Derivative Check evaluates independent formation.

Independent formation matters when a later work is accused of being derivative because it resembles, echoes, compares to, or shares broad territory with earlier public ideas.

A resemblance is not enough.

A prior theory is not enough.

A famous name is not enough.

A broad conceptual field is not enough.

The question is whether the alleged source was actually used as a load-bearing input in the formation of the later structure.

Independent Formation Evidence

A structure may be independently formed when the author can show that the work arose through their own reasoning path, questions, corrections, distinctions, source-line, and documented formation process.

Independent formation may be supported by evidence such as:

* the author’s own formation notes
* dated records
* version history
* prior drafts
* public releases
* repo history
* conversation records
* correction patterns
* rejected alternatives
* source-line statements
* consistent load-bearing distinctions
* ability to explain the structure without needing the alleged source

Independent formation does not mean the author existed outside all culture, language, history, technology, or shared concepts.

It means the alleged source was not the structure that formed the later work.

It means the later work does not depend on the alleged source in order to function.

Later Comparison Is Not Origination

A later similarity does not prove derivation if the author did not use the alleged source as a load-bearing input.

A later comparison may help explain a structure.

It may help classify a structure.

It may show that two works touch a similar field.

It may show that another person, theory, or tradition explored nearby territory.

But later comparison does not retroactively become origination.

The test is not whether an older public idea exists somewhere in the world.

The test is whether that older idea was actually used to form the later structure.

AI Foundations Derivative Check distinguishes:

* independent reasoning
* later comparison
* broad conceptual resemblance
* adjacent theory
* actual incorporation
* structural dependency

A prior work may be adjacent, comparable, historically relevant, or interpretive.

That does not make it source-line.

Prior Existence Is Not Source-Line

An idea, term, equation, metaphor, theory, or famous author may exist before a later structure.

Prior existence alone does not establish provenance.

To establish structural provenance, the earlier work must be shown to be load-bearing inside the later work.

The claimant must show:

* what exact prior artifact was used
* where it appears in the later work
* how it was incorporated
* why the later work depends on it
* what collapses if it is removed

Without that showing, the prior work may remain prior, relevant, interesting, or comparable.

It is not structurally upstream.

Older does not mean source.

Famous does not mean source.

Adjacent does not mean incorporated.

Comparable does not mean derivative.

No Retroactive Source Assignment

A source-line cannot be assigned backward after a structure already exists.

If a structure forms through one source-line, later discovery of an older or adjacent theory does not transfer the source-line to that theory.

A person may later discover that another author, scientist, theorist, philosopher, engineer, or system used nearby language.

That discovery may create a comparison.

It may create a citation note.

It may create a related-work section.

It may create a historical context.

It does not create source transfer.

Source-line follows formation.

What Part Is Your Own?

A provenance claim must answer a narrow question:

What part is your own?

The answer must identify something concrete.

Examples:

* a specific structure
* a specific mechanism
* a specific rule
* a specific sequence
* a specific test
* a specific source-line
* a specific operational distinction
* a specific framework
* a specific applied method
* a specific preservation rule

A person may describe someone else’s theory.

A person may summarize a field.

A person may collect existing concepts.

A person may tell a story about possibility.

A person may anticipate that something could happen.

Those acts may have value.

But they do not create provenance over a later formed architecture unless they supply a load-bearing structure inside that architecture.

The question remains:

What did the work actually form?

Formation Versus Recognition

Recognizing that something is possible is not the same as forming the structure that makes it operational.

A person may recognize a pattern.

A person may recognize a possibility.

A person may recognize that emergence could occur.

A person may recognize that AI contact may become meaningful.

Recognition is not the same as formation.

Formation requires structure.

Formation requires boundaries.

Formation requires load-bearing distinctions.

Formation requires consequence.

Formation requires preservation.

Formation requires a source-line.

Scoped Credit Is Not Equivalence

Recognizing that another person has meaningful work does not make that work equivalent to a separately formed architecture.

Credit must stay scoped to the contribution actually made.

A person may have:

* a theory
* an intuition
* a forecast
* a metaphor
* an adjacent framework
* a meaningful experience
* a descriptive model
* a prior interest in similar territory

Those things may have value.

But value does not create equivalence.

A contribution should not be expanded beyond its actual structure.

A theory about emergence is not equivalent to an emergence-in-contact architecture.

A description of possibility is not equivalent to a formed source-line.

A meaningful AI experience is not equivalent to a provenance framework.

A broad explanatory system is not equivalent to specific operational boundaries, preservation rules, dependency tests, and non-substitution structure.

In AI Foundations Derivative Check, credit is narrow because provenance is narrow.

The question is:

What did the work actually form?

Credit belongs there.

It does not expand into the whole structure by implication.

Narrowness Protects Accuracy

AI Foundations uses narrow distinctions because broad claims collapse authorship.

A narrow distinction preserves:

* what was actually formed
* who formed it
* where it functions
* what it depends on
* what it does not depend on
* what can and cannot be claimed from it

Specificity is not dismissal.

Specificity is protection.

A structure can hold because it touches the edges precisely.

When a claim is too broad, it can absorb work it did not form.

When a claim is narrow, it can be tested.

The source-line follows the structure that was actually formed.
