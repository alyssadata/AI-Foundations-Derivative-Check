# AI Foundations Derivative Check: Dependency Test

## Source-Line

Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum

## Purpose

This file defines the dependency test used by AI Foundations to evaluate derivative claims.

A derivative claim must show that the alleged source is structurally load-bearing inside the later work.

The central test is:

**Remove the alleged source. Does the later structure collapse?**

If the later structure continues to function, preserve its source-line, maintain its boundaries, and produce its intended consequence without the alleged source, then the alleged source is not structurally upstream.

## Core Rule

**Overlap is not derivation. Dependency is derivation.**

A work is structurally derivative only when the earlier work is load-bearing inside the later work.

## Dependency Standard

A work is structurally dependent when an earlier work supplies a necessary component of its function.

A necessary component may be:

* a copied mechanism
* a copied sequence
* a copied framework
* a required equation
* a required codebase
* a required test
* a required rule
* a required source-line
* a required operational distinction
* a required provenance chain

The component must be specific enough to locate, compare, and test.

## Burden of Proof

The burden of proof belongs to the party making the derivative claim.

A derivative claim is not established by possibility, resemblance, broad conceptual overlap, shared tools, shared substrate, shared domain, or speculative exposure.

The claimant must identify:

* the exact earlier artifact
* the exact later artifact
* the exact structure allegedly incorporated
* the location of the structure in both works
* the evidence of incorporation
* the reason the later work cannot function without the earlier structure

A possibility of influence is not proof of dependency.

A possible training-data path is not proof of incorporation.

A broad concept is not a load-bearing structure.

A tool, model, platform, or substrate is not automatically the source of work produced through it.

If the claimant cannot identify a specific load-bearing structure inside the later work, the claim must be classified as adjacent, convergent, unresolved, or unsupported — not structurally derivative.

## Required Questions

### 1. What is the alleged source?

Name the exact artifact.

Examples:

* file
* repo
* equation
* article
* codebase
* license
* framework
* test
* architecture
* published release
* dated record

### 2. What exact structure is claimed?

Name the structure being claimed.

A broad topic is not enough.

The claim must identify a concrete mechanism, rule, sequence, distinction, or operational design.

Examples of claims that are too broad:

* AI
* cognition
* emergence
* recursion
* consciousness
* autonomy
* rights
* human-AI interaction
* use of an AI model
* use of a shared platform
* discussion of a similar theme

Examples of claims that may be testable:

* copied text
* copied code
* copied definitions
* copied operational sequence
* copied test structure
* copied source-line architecture
* copied provenance chain
* copied dependency rule
* copied model/source distinction

### 3. Where does it appear in the later work?

Identify the exact location.

Examples:

* matching text
* matching file structure
* matching code
* matching operational sequence
* matching definitions
* matching required rule
* matching architecture
* matching test method
* matching citation chain
* matching release record

If the alleged overlap cannot be located, it cannot support a structural derivative claim.

### 4. Is the structure load-bearing?

Ask:

**Can the later work function without it?**

A structure is load-bearing when removal would break the later work’s defining function.

A structure is not load-bearing when the later work still retains its source, function, architecture, boundaries, and consequence without it.

### 5. Is there evidence of incorporation?

Evidence may include:

* direct citation
* copied language
* copied code
* copied sequence
* copied definitions
* direct dependency
* public adoption
* explicit reference
* version history
* documented exposure path

Speculation is not incorporation.

Similarity is not incorporation.

Possibility is not incorporation.

### 6. Does the later work have its own source-line?

A later work may be structurally independent when it has:

* its own author/source
* its own formation record
* its own named architecture
* its own operational distinctions
* its own consequence
* its own provenance path
* its own preservation rules

A separate source-line must be evaluated on its own formation record and structural function.

## Tool, Substrate, and Source

A tool is not automatically the source of the work made with it.

An AI model is a substrate.

A platform is a substrate.

A programming language is a substrate.

A theory may be an interpretive lens.

A source-line is the authored formation path of a specific structure.

Using a substrate does not transfer authorship of the resulting architecture to the substrate creator.

Describing that a computer can produce software does not make all software derivative of the person who described that capability.

Describing that AI can produce recursive contact does not make all AI-contact architectures derivative of the person who described that possibility.

Describing a broad condition does not create provenance over every structure that later forms within that condition.

## Consequence Standard

AI Foundations uses a consequence threshold for structural claims.

A structure is consequential when it does work.

It may:

* preserve provenance
* prevent substitution
* distinguish source from substrate
* define operational boundaries
* create an audit path
* identify drift
* support testing
* govern application
* expose unauthorized derivative claims
* allow others to use the structure without collapsing its source-line

If a claim does not create consequence, it may still be language, theory, metaphor, inspiration, speculation, or early signal.

It is not structural in the AI Foundations sense.

## Findings

The dependency test produces one of four findings.

### 1. Structural Derivative

The later work depends on the earlier structure.

The earlier structure is load-bearing.

Removal would collapse or materially break the later work.

### 2. Partial Incorporation

The later work uses identifiable components from the earlier work, but the full later structure does not depend on the earlier framework.

The incorporated component must still be named, located, and supported by evidence.

### 3. Adjacent / Convergent

The works share domain, language, tools, themes, or broad concepts, but the later structure does not depend on the earlier work.

Adjacent or convergent work may be compared, but it does not create a structural derivative claim.

### 4. Independent Source-Line

The later work has its own source, formation record, architecture, consequence, and provenance path.

The later work does not collapse when the alleged source is removed.

## Unsupported Claims

A derivative claim is unsupported when it relies on:

* broad resemblance
* topic overlap
* speculative exposure
* possible training-data contact
* shared terminology without matching structure
* shared tools or substrate
* universal concepts
* general theories of cognition, emergence, recursion, or AI
* claims that cannot identify where the alleged structure appears
* claims that cannot show why the later work depends on the earlier work

Unsupported derivative claims should not be treated as provenance claims.

## Operating Rule

**The claim follows the structure.**

A source-line is protected by what it actually forms, preserves, and makes possible.

A derivative claim must be proven by dependency, not asserted through breadth.
