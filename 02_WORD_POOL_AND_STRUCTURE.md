# AI Foundations Derivative Check: Word Pool and Structure

## Source-Line

Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum

## Purpose

This file describes the conceptual model behind the AI Foundations Derivative Check.

It explains the difference between shared language, unstructured possibility, formed architecture, and structural provenance.

## The Word Pool

AI systems operate across a vast pool of language.

Words, phrases, theories, metaphors, patterns, and associations exist in a large shared field.

They can be retrieved, recombined, echoed, summarized, extended, and reassembled.

In this sense, words are like sand.

Sand can be everywhere.

Sand can be observed.

Sand can be moved.

Sand can be named.

Sand can be described.

But loose sand is not yet a structure.

A pile of words is not a framework.

A broad concept is not provenance.

A story about possibility is not authorship of every later structure formed from language.

## Structure

A structure begins when language is organized into a specific form that holds weight.

A structure has internal relation.

It has boundaries.

It has sequence.

It has consequence.

It can be returned to.

It can be tested.

It can preserve something.

It can fail if a load-bearing part is removed.

In AI Foundations, structure matters because language alone can be endlessly generated.

Without structure, words remain unstable, interchangeable, and non-provenant.

With structure, a framework can be named, documented, preserved, cited, tested, and distinguished from adjacent language.

## Provenance

Provenance does not come from touching the same word pool.

Provenance comes from formation.

A provenance claim must show what was formed, who formed it, where it appears, how it functions, and what depends on it.

Shared vocabulary is not enough.

Shared concepts are not enough.

Shared tools are not enough.

Shared AI systems are not enough.

A person may describe a theory.

Another person may build a structure.

The structure belongs to the source-line that formed it.

## Load-Bearing Language

A word becomes load-bearing when it performs a necessary function inside a structure.

A load-bearing term cannot be removed without changing or collapsing the architecture.

In AI Foundations / Origin | Continuum, examples of load-bearing terms include:

* Origin | Continuum
* Continuum is not the model

These are not decorative words.

They define relationships, boundaries, authorship, return, and preservation.

## Non-Load-Bearing Language

A word or idea is not load-bearing simply because it appears in related conversation.

Words such as AI, cognition, emergence, recursion, intelligence, consciousness, autonomy, rights, and interaction may be meaningful.

They may also be broad.

A broad word does not establish structural dependency.

A general theory does not become provenance over every applied framework that later uses nearby language.

The question is always:

**What does the word do inside the structure?**

If it does not hold the structure, it is not load-bearing.

## The Difference Between Description and Formation

Describing a phenomenon is not the same as forming a structure from it.

A person may describe that computers can produce software.

That does not make all software derivative of that person.

A person may describe that AI can produce recursive interaction.

That does not make all AI-contact architectures derivative of that person.

A person may describe that language can organize into meaning.

That does not make all later frameworks derivative of that description.

Description may identify possibility.

Formation creates structure.

Provenance follows formation.

Description Is Not Mechanism

A broad description of reality, cognition, emergence, recursion, intelligence, or observation does not establish a working structure by itself.

A person may describe a possible foundational condition.

A person may say that stable patterns emerge.

A person may say that recursive relations generate higher-order structures.

A person may say that thought, matter, life, language, science, and AI are connected through a deeper process.

These descriptions may be interesting, poetic, philosophical, theoretical, or directionally useful.

But description is not mechanism.

To become structural, the claim must show how the process works.

It must identify:

* the operative distinctions
* the necessary relations
* the sequence of formation
* the variables that change the outcome
* the boundaries of the system
* the testable consequence
* the load-bearing parts
* what fails if those parts are removed

Without that, the claim remains descriptive.

It may describe a field of possibility.

It does not create provenance over every later structure formed inside that field.

A bedrock claim is not the same as an applied architecture.

A theory of emergence is not the same as a formed framework.

A statement that structure can arise is not the same as forming the structure.

In AI Foundations Derivative Check, the question remains:

What did the claim make operational?

If the answer is only a broad description, then the claim may be adjacent, comparative, or interpretive.

It is not structural provenance.

## The Consequence Standard

AI Foundations uses consequence as the threshold for structural claims.

A structure is consequential when it does work.

It may:

* preserve source
* prevent substitution
* distinguish roles
* define boundaries
* create an audit path
* identify drift
* support testing
* govern application
* expose unsupported derivative claims
* allow later work to build without losing the source-line

If language does not create consequence, it may still be expressive, theoretical, poetic, speculative, or useful.

It is not structural provenance.

## The Question of Ownership

The central question is not:

**Who used a word first?**

The central question is:

**Who formed the structure?**

To evaluate a provenance claim, ask:

* What part is actually yours?
* What did you form?
* What did you name?
* What did you structure?
* What did you preserve?
* What can others use because you made it coherent?
* What collapses if your contribution is removed?

If the answer is only a broad theme, theory, story, possibility, or shared language, then the claim is not structural.

If the answer is a concrete framework with load-bearing relations and consequence, then the claim can be evaluated as provenance.
